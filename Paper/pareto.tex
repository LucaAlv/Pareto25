\begin{abstract}

This seminar paper presents a microlevel study, using individual-level panel data from Mexico combined with frequent occurrence of natural disasters and significant climatic variations, thus providing a quasi-experiment to examine the influence of extreme weather events on migration. Using data on x households in year x, y in year y, etc. we analyze whole-household/individual municipality-to-municipality migration, which allows us to understand the effects of environmental factors on permanten moves that may differ from temporary migration. The results suggest that the episodic disasters that serve as the main treatment variable have a very small or no impact on long-term migration. These findings on the minimal influence of natural disasters on permantent moves are supplemented by additional research on short term migration using the facebook data. 
These country-specific findings highlight the contextually-specific
nature of climate-migration relationships

\end{abstract}
